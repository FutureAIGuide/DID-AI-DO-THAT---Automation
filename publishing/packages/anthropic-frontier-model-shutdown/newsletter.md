## Preview Text

A U.S. restriction aimed at foreign nationals caused a worldwide model shutdown. The public still cannot inspect the government's technical case.

## Opening

At 5:21 p.m. on June 12, Anthropic says it received a U.S. government directive covering foreign-national access to its newest AI models. Within hours, Claude Fable 5 and Claude Mythos 5 were unavailable to everyone.

The models had launched three days earlier.

## What We Know

Anthropic's public statement says the directive applied to every foreign national, including foreign-national employees inside the United States. The company responded by disabling both models globally.

Fable 5 was designed for broad use with automated safeguards. Mythos 5 used the same underlying model but offered fewer cyber restrictions to vetted Project Glasswing organizations.

Anthropic had described Mythos-class models as unusually powerful cybersecurity systems capable of finding and exploiting difficult software vulnerabilities. It also said no developer had created perfect jailbreak resistance.

## What We Do Not Know

The directive is not public. Neither is the government's complete technical evidence.

Anthropic says the apparent jailbreak demonstrated only narrow capabilities available from other public models. The government may possess additional or classified evidence, but it has not publicly explained the standard it applied.

That means the public cannot yet determine whether the shutdown stopped an exceptional threat or removed a defensive tool through an unnecessarily broad intervention.

## Why It Matters

The incident may become a precedent for controlling access to cloud-hosted frontier models through private export-control orders.

It also exposes a policy contradiction. Days before the shutdown, the White House ordered faster adoption of frontier AI for cybersecurity and national security. The same government then triggered the withdrawal of Anthropic's strongest systems from vetted defenders and everyone else.

Those positions could be reconciled by a clear, consistently applied technical standard. No such public standard currently explains the June 12 decision.

## The Central Question

Who should decide when a frontier model is too dangerous to release, and how much evidence should the public receive when the detailed risk must remain secret?
